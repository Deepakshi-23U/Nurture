Dog vaccination schedule

Vaccination plays a crucial role in safeguarding the health of your canine companion, shielding them from potentially life-threatening diseases. Starting as early as 6-8 weeks old, puppies receive their initial vaccinations, typically against core diseases like rabies, distemper, parvovirus, and adenovirus. These initial shots are often followed by booster doses every 3-4 weeks until around 16 weeks of age, ensuring robust immunity during their vulnerable early months.

As dogs mature, annual booster shots or titers are recommended to maintain their defenses against infectious diseases. The core vaccines are considered essential for all dogs due to the severe health risks associated with the diseases they prevent. However, non-core vaccines may also be administered based on individual lifestyle and risk factors, such as exposure to ticks, mosquitoes, or other specific environmental conditions.

Consulting with a veterinarian is paramount to develop a tailored vaccination schedule that aligns with your dog's unique needs and circumstances. Vets can assess factors like geographical location, local disease prevalence, and your dog's overall health to provide personalized recommendations. By staying up-to-date with vaccinations, you not only protect your beloved pet but also contribute to the overall health and well-being of the canine community.

Helping Injured Animals


Common injuries requiring first-aid for animals are bite wounds, gunshot wounds, or injuries from being hit by a car.

The first thing to do, whether helping a pet or a wild animal, is to make sure the animal cannot hurt any person.

Any time an animal is injured, it may be in pain and fearful, so it may try to bite you, especially if you try to touch the part that is hurt.

With dogs, a soft cord or long strips of gauze wrapped around the muzzle and tied behind the head prevents it from snapping. The animal then can be wrapped in a large blanket and taken to the veterinarian.

A good way to transport an injured animal is wrapped in a blanket and placed in an airline carrier. Being quiet and covering the animal's eyes calms the animal and makes it easier to handle.

If the animal is unconscious, treat it as you would a possible spinal injury for a person. Wrap the animal on a board so its legs, spine, and neck are stiff and take it to the veterinarian.

Especially with an unconscious animal, make sure the airway is clear and breathing is not blocked. Use a cloth to clear the mouth and protect your hand from possible bites.

If an animal is bleeding, the first-aid treatment is the same for animals and humans. Apply steady pressure with gauze or a clean towel to limit the blood flow.

Pet care: Dos and don'ts you must follow for your pet's well-being



Dos:
• For both cats and dogs, make sure you provide two dishes, one for food and one for water and they should be quite separate from each other
• It is preferable to feed pets with pet food that is specifically designed to fulfill their nutritional needs rather than human food
• Pets should always be fed in clean bowls. To ensure this, one should use steel or ceramic bowls as they can be cleaned easily
• Make sure that your pet has access to fresh and filtered water at all times
• Make sure to offer a nutritionally complete meal that has been formulated to meet the unique nutritional demands of each breed, age, activity level and sensitivities as well as any medical conditions

Don'ts:
• Avoid feeding your pets with excessive treats as it may fill them but will not be providing the nutrition needed by their body
• Never give chocolate to your pet because it might be harmful to their health
• Keep your pet away from heat-absorbing surfaces like metal or asphalt. Animals can burn their paws and the heat from their feet can also increase their core body temperature.
